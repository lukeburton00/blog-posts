# Three Lessons From My First Year as a Software Engineer

As a child, I was obsessed with video games. When I realized the games I played didn't just appear out of nowhere but rather were developed by _actual people_, my obsession took a more creative turn. Though the majority of my projects as a teenager were spectacular failures, the obsession remained, and I've wanted to work as a programmer ever since. This past September, I completed my first year as a software engineer at Breeze Airways. Getting to this point has been a dream come true, but the work is only just beginning, and I look forward to every challenge to come. In the meantime, there are three main takeaways from my first year in the field that I'll be sharing in this post.

### 1. Learning is a personal matter.

For the first month of my job at Breeze, I was assigned a mentor from the team. Every day, we'd meet for a few minutes to cover the task I was working on, any questions or blockers I had, and where I should go next. I was ramping up fast, and my confidence grew quickly.

Soon enough, the month was over, and I was on my own. The _smart_ thing to do at this point would have been to continue to reach out to others for help at a similar consistency to my daily meetings with my mentor, to seek out plenty of pairing sessions, and overall to be seeking feedback and guidance wherever I could get it. However, I allowed my early confidence to take over, seeking to get through tasks without input and to prove to myself and my manager that I could get things done without help. The results were, predictably, disastrous. I blocked others often and slowed down team's processes. I had become afraid of asking questions.

I had the expectation that learning in this job would be handed to me, that it would just come with time. I assumed my manager knew I wanted interesting and challenging work, and that he'd assign me to it right away. I continued to pick up every low hanging fruit on the to-do board, avoiding situations where I knew I would need to reach out for help.

It took a few weeks, longer than it should've, for me to accept that my progress was up to me. I began to ask to be added to tasks that sounded interesting, even if I had no idea how to get them done. I reached out to the team with every question I had, even if it felt like it should be obvious. In time, this allowed me to step out of my comfort zone and get back to making real progress. I wish I had taken my learning into my own hands earlier, but I'm glad I learned to actively seek it out rather than hide behind comfort.

### 2. Complain less, document more.

Onboarding onto the team was a painful process. The existing documentation hadn't been updated in at least a year and only covered half the tools employed by the team. Where a write-up had been put together, context was limited. It wasn't clear what pieces of the dev environment depended on others. In one of my first one-on-one meetings with my manager, I let him know how frustrating this process was. His response was humbling, and important.

"Fix it."

He explained that nobody had better context for the onboarding process than the newest hire. Rather than let these issues be put off and cause frustration for the next new developer on the team, he encouraged me to revise existing docs and create new ones for any pain points in the process. At first this sounded like busywork, or at least meant for somebody who had more understanding of the team's processes than me. But I've since learned that not only is good documentation a responsibility for every engineer, it's also a huge opportunity to make a meaningful impact.

Since that meeting, I've adopted a personal rule: any time I need to go looking for documentation about the team's processes, tools, standards, or other shared knowledge and I can't find it, I write it myself. I've had the chance to fill in the gaps and make connections between disparate knowledge bases. My own understanding of various tools and workflows has improved. I've seen the impact it's had for new hires. I've even noticed a bit of a culture shift on the team. Documentation is now more likely to be updated when processes change, and shared knowledge is less likely to stay tribal. I'd like to think my conversation with my manager and the work that followed played a small role in this change.
